# Digitized Books and Archives

Free access to out-of-copyright genealogy books, county histories, and compiled records. Platforms: Archive.org, Google Books, HathiTrust.

## Overview

Genealogy books published before approximately 1928 are out of copyright and freely digitized. County histories (1880-1920), family genealogies, published vital records abstracts, and compiled church records are available at no cost. This is Tier 3 evidence — but the books themselves often compiled from Tier 1-2 records that are otherwise unavailable, destroyed, or un-digitized.

Before declaring a pre-1900 US ancestor a brick wall: search Archive.org for county histories covering their location. These books routinely name 3-4 generations of family members in biographical sketches.

## Archive.org (Internet Archive)

`https://archive.org/`

**Access**: Free for out-of-copyright works (public domain, generally pre-1928). Books published after ~1928 require borrowing — free account, 1-hour loan, limited simultaneous borrows.

**Search**:
```
https://archive.org/search?query={QUERY}&mediatype=texts
```

**Full text search within a book**: navigate to `https://archive.org/details/{IDENTIFIER}/mode/2up` and use the browser's text search on the OCR text layer.

**What's here for genealogy**
- County histories: "History of [County] County, [State]" — 19th and early 20th century. Name every prominent family, often with biographical sketches naming parents and grandparents.
- Family genealogies: compiled family histories, often with primary source citations.
- Abstract books: published abstracts of wills, deeds, and court records organized by county.
- Church records: published transcriptions of early church registers.

**High-value books for US genealogy** (illustrative examples of what to search for)
- *Chalkley's Chronicles* (Augusta County, VA) — abstracts of court records, deeds, wills organized by surname
- County histories published by Chapman Publishing, Goodspeed Publishing, and Lewis Publishing Co. 1880-1920 — all follow the same biographical sketch format
- Any "*[Surname] Family in America*" or "*[Surname] Genealogy*" compiled family history

**Search strategy**: search for the county history by location first (`"history of [county] county [state]"`), then do a full-text search within the book for the target surname.

## Google Books

`https://books.google.com/`

**Access**: Free snippet view for most books. Full view for out-of-copyright works.

**Key use**: full-text search across millions of scanned books simultaneously — finds surname mentions across county histories and compiled genealogies. More effective than Archive.org for discovering whether a family name is mentioned anywhere.

**Effective search syntax**:
```
"[surname]" "[county]" "[state]" genealogy site:books.google.com
```
Or directly in Google Books advanced search with date range set to pre-1928.

**FHL Film Number cross-reference**: Google Books often surfaces book titles that are also on FamilySearch Catalog. Note the book title, then find the microfilm via FS Catalog for a higher-quality scan or a page the Google Books scan missed.

## HathiTrust

`https://catalog.hathitrust.org/`

**Access**: Full-text search for all books (including in-copyright). Full view and download for out-of-copyright. Affiliated library access for some copyrighted works.

**Advantage over Google Books**: more complete OCR indexing, better advanced search, can restrict by date range and language, does not de-rank historical books in favor of commercial results.

**Search**:
```
https://catalog.hathitrust.org/Search/Home?lookfor={QUERY}&type=all
```

Add `&filter[]=publishDate:[1800 TO 1928]` to restrict to out-of-copyright works.

**Good for**: German-American and Scandinavian-American genealogy books, church conference minutes, ethnic community histories — categories where HathiTrust's university library holdings exceed Google Books.

## FHL Film Number Hack (Cross-Platform)

Ancestry index records often include an FHL (Family History Library) film number in the source citation. Copy that number and search the FamilySearch Catalog:

```
https://www.familysearch.org/search/catalog
```

Search by film number. This finds the original unindexed microfilm that produced the Ancestry index entry — often contains adjacent records on surrounding pages that were never indexed. Useful for finding siblings, neighbors, and related entries that did not get pulled into the Ancestry index.

## Evidence Tiers

| Source Type | Tier | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Compiled genealogy with cited primary sources | 3 | Verify each citation against the original record |
| County history with family biographical sketches | 3-4 | Often compiled from family-supplied information; treat as lead |
| Published will/deed abstracts | 2-3 | Abstracts may omit details; verify against courthouse originals |
| Published church register transcriptions | 2-3 | Quality varies significantly by transcriber and era |

**Evidence usage rule**: A county history naming a person's parents is a lead (Tier 3-4), not proof. Follow each claim back to a primary source. The value of these books is discovering what primary sources to look for — not substituting for them.

## Finding Aids for Genealogy Book Searches

**WorldCat** (`https://www.worldcat.org/`): find which libraries hold a specific genealogy title. If a book is not on Archive.org, Google Books, or HathiTrust, WorldCat identifies which library owns it and whether interlibrary loan is available.

**Allen County Public Library Genealogy Center** (`https://www.genealogycenter.org/`): holds the largest genealogy book collection in the US. Their online catalog identifies books available for onsite use if travel is possible.

**Genealogical Society of Utah (GSU) film catalog**: the FamilySearch Catalog is the searchable index. Many compiled genealogies were microfilmed and are accessible at FamilySearch Family History Centers worldwide.
