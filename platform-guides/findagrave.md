# Find A Grave Platform Guide

## Overview

Find A Grave is most valuable for three things: exact death dates, burial locations, and relationships named on memorial inscriptions. Treat it as the last stop in a research sequence, not the first. Its coverage is uneven — strong for 1865–1950 American burials, sparse for pre-1850 records outside New England, and crowdsourced in ways that require verification.

---

## Source Tier Assessment

FAG data is not a uniform tier. Evaluate each piece of information independently:

| Evidence type | Tier | Notes |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Memorial inscription naming relationships ("beloved husband of", "father of") | 2–3 | Often the only record explicitly naming the relationship |
| Death date with citation to a death certificate or newspaper obit | 2–3 | Treat like any cited source |
| Death date with no source citation | 5 | Corroboration only — never standalone support |
| Linked spouse or child memorials | 5 | Crowdsourced pointers, not confirmed relationships |
| Birth year on a gravestone | 3–4 | Engraved at burial time, may reflect family memory |

The default for unsourced FAG dates is Tier 5. Assume an unsourced date is someone's best guess transcribed from a stone that may itself be approximate. Use only for corroboration against other evidence.

---

## Searching for Married Women

FAG memorials use the final married surname, not maiden name. Searching by maiden name will return nothing for women who married (or remarried after widowhood).

Workaround sequence:
1. Check SSDI first for the death-record surname — SSDI uses the surname at time of death
2. Search FAG by the married surname found in SSDI
3. For pre-SSDI (pre-1936 deaths), check the obituary or death certificate first if available

Also try abbreviated first-name variants. FAG transcriptions and memorial creators frequently use nicknames or abbreviations that differ from birth names:
- "Armantha" → "Mantha"
- "Elizabeth" → "Betsy" or "Eliza"
- "Wilhelmina" → "Minnie"
- "Margaret" → "Maggie" or "Peggy"

Search all known name variants before concluding the person has no FAG memorial.

## Memorial Inscriptions

Inscription text is the most valuable content on FAG. Text like "beloved husband of Mary Jones Smith" or "daughter of John and Catherine Williams" is Tier 2–3 evidence for relationships — often the only extant record that explicitly names the relationship.

Stones carved near the time of death by family members who knew the deceased are more reliable than stones erected decades later. Note the carving style and apparent age of the stone in your source documentation when this is visible from the photo.

For pre-1870 persons, inscription text may be the only surviving documentation of a relationship. Record it precisely, including spelling variants.

---

## Linked Memorial Relationships

FAG links spouses and children within the memorial interface. These links are created by volunteer contributors and carry no source requirement. Treat all linked-memorial relationships as Tier 5 pointers:
- Use them to find other memorials worth investigating
- Verify each claimed relationship against independent evidence before recording it
- Do not treat a linked child memorial as evidence of parentage

Common errors: wrong person linked (same name, different generation), spouse links across two different marriages without clarity, children linked from the wrong family line when names are common.

---

## Transcription Quality

FAG transcriptions are crowdsourced and frequently contain errors, particularly for:
- Faded or worn limestone stones
- Ornate script (common in Victorian-era markers)
- German, Latin, or non-English inscriptions
- Stones with multiple dates (born/died vs. baptized/confirmed)

If a transcription seems inconsistent with other known data — wrong birth year by a decade, impossible date sequence, wrong county — request the original photo. Many memorials have photos attached. If the photo exists and the transcription conflicts with the visible inscription, the photo wins.

If no photo is available and the transcription is the only data, flag it as uncertain.

## When FAG is Most Useful

Search FAG after FS and WikiTree, not before. FAG adds value in specific scenarios:

**Best use cases:**
- Finding burial location (narrows the county or parish for probate and church record searches)
- Exact death date for persons missing from SSDI (pre-1936 or uncovered workers)
- Pre-1900 persons with visible inscribed stones, particularly in New England and mid-Atlantic states
- Confirming relationships via inscription text when no other record names them

**Weak coverage:**
- Pre-1850 persons outside New England — FAG data is sparse
- Pre-1865 Southern records — many records and cemeteries were not indexed
- Enslaved persons — systematic undercoverage, though some freedmen-era cemeteries have been indexed

**Platform sequence**: Ancestry → FamilySearch → WikiTree → Find A Grave → Geni → Chronicling America. FAG answers specific narrow questions; it is not a starting point.

## FAG and Death Date Corroboration

A specific workflow: if you have a death year from a census ("widowed as of 1880, married in 1870 census") but no exact date, FAG can narrow it. Search the person, find the memorial, and check whether the stone gives a month and day. Even an unsourced Tier 5 death date narrows the window for searching newspaper obituaries and death certificates, which are Tier 1–2 sources.

The FAG date is a research lead, not a conclusion. Follow it to the primary record.
